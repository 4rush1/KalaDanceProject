About Us

Guru Usha Bhatnagar:


The teacher of this class is Usha Bhatnagar, who at the age of ten, won the “All India” national dance competition for her Kathak dance performance. She initially learnt the Jaipur Gharana from Guru Shankar Dev Jha, but in her adulthood, learnt the Lucknow Gharana from a very prestigious and famous Kathak dancer, Pandit Shambhu Maharaj. His ancestors were the people who formed the Lucknow Gharana, it is like a family heirloom passed down to every generation. When Usha auditioned to be in Pandit Shambhu Maharaj’s class, he began crying as he said: “you were born to dance, what took you so long to come to me, where have you been my whole life?”. After this, her Kathak career took off and she began dancing in many national conferences with famous musicians. In Aotearoa, it is tough to find a dance class that teaches traditional Kathak. Nowadays, most Kathak classes have mixed various Gharanas together and formed more of a modern, contemporary dance form. With the Kala dance group, you can learn traditional Kathak Gharanas and preserve this ancient dance form.

The history of Kathak:


Kathak dates back to the 4th century BC. The word “Katha” means story, and Kathakars were storytellers who often danced as a form of worship or devotion. Kathak is the only North Indian classical dance form, and also the only Indian classical dance that has both Hindu and Islamic cultural influences (due Mughal empires). Like all classical Indian dance forms, Kathak dance moves don’t vary based on gender/sex.

During the British Raj, most Indian dance forms (including Kathak) were discouraged, as the British thought they were demonic and vulgar. Kathak was nearly erased by the British, however, it managed to stay alive thanks to certain families who secretly developed the dance within their homes.From this, three distinct variations of Kathak to formed (known as Gharanas), named after the cities they originated from: Lucknow, Banaras, and Jaipur.
